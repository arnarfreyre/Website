Understanding Our Forest Giants

Trees in warm biomes represent Earth's most diverse and ecologically significant plant communities, harboring approximately 73,300 species and providing ecosystem services worth trillions of dollars annually.

At Arbor Academy, we focus on these critical ecosystems that sequester 95% of global tree-based carbon and support 62% of terrestrial vertebrate species. Our research reveals how warm biome trees directly sustain over one billion people while facing unprecedented threats from deforestation and climate change.

Carbon Sequestration Studies

Tropical trees alone are responsible for 95% of all tree-based CO₂ sequestration globally, with individual trees sequestering a minimum of 50kg carbon annually. Planted forests can remove 4.5-40.7 tonnes CO₂ per hectare yearly.

Learn More

Biodiversity in Forest Canopies

A single hectare of tropical rainforest can support over 480 tree species—compared to just 6 in temperate forests. One Peruvian Amazon tree can host more ant species than the entire British Isles.

Learn More

Tree Communication Networks

Trees allocate 13.12 Gt CO₂e annually to mycorrhizal fungi—equivalent to 36% of fossil fuel emissions. These networks enable nutrient sharing and create the foundation for forest resilience.

Warm Biome Adaptations

  • Hydraulic Systems: Leaf-specific conductivity increases with height (r²=0.50), enabling water transport >40m
  • Deep Roots: Access water at 3.3m depth, contributing 21-90% to transpiration during drought
  • Transpiration: Large trees can transpire 1,600 liters daily, cooling surroundings by 1-3°C
  • Photosynthesis: 95% use C3, with C4 and CAM pathways providing advantages in specific environments

Tropical Dry Forests

Conservation Challenges & Solutions

Urgent action needed to protect our forest heritage

Current Threats

6.7M hectares Primary forest lost in 2024
  • 90% of global deforestation in tropical forests
  • 70-80% driven by agricultural expansion
  • 110% increase in Amazon fire-related loss (2024)
  • 11.6% average extinction rate in biodiversity hotspots

Conservation Success

Brazil achieved 84% reduction in Amazon deforestation (2004-2012) through improved monitoring

  • REDD+: 65 countries engaged, $5.2B pledged
  • Community management reduces poverty while maintaining forests
  • 26% of emissions offset by tropical forest regeneration
  • Costa Rica reversed forest loss through ecosystem payments

Climate Change Impact

Temperature increases exceeding 1.5°C combined with 10-20% rainfall reductions push forests toward critical tipping points. The Southeast Amazon's dry season has extended from 4 to 5 months, threatening irreversible ecosystem transitions.
